Where New Mexico bills go to die

By Amy Traylor, Editor & Publisher · July 10, 2026

Most bills introduced in the New Mexico Legislature never become law. That much is widely understood. Less visible is where they stop — and our database of every bill from 1996 through 2026 makes the answer unusually concrete.

Of the roughly 29,700 bills in the record that died, the overwhelming majority — about 26,800 — died in a committee, never reaching a floor vote. The Legislature's committees are not a formality on the way to the floor. For most legislation, they are the destination.

The two great bottlenecks

Two committees stand apart, and it is no accident that both control money. Senate Finance has had more bills referred to it than any other committee in the database — 15,423 — and roughly 2,870 of them died there. The House Appropriations & Finance Committee is close behind: 11,733 bills referred, about 2,736 dead. Nearly anything that spends money, or touches something that does, is routed through one of these two rooms, and thousands of bills reach them and go no further.

Their kill rates — 19% for Senate Finance, 23% for House Appropriations & Finance — are actually lower than several other committees'. What makes them decisive is volume: when almost every consequential bill must pass through a single committee, even a modest death rate stops a great deal of legislation.

Where the odds are worst

Measured by the share of bills that die there, the toughest rooms are elsewhere. Senate Education ends about 36% of the bills referred to it. House Education and Senate Public Affairs each kill roughly 30%. Senate and House Judiciary land in between. A bill sent to one of these committees faces materially worse odds than the raw statewide average.

Why it matters

Committees are where the fewest people can stop the most legislation, often without a recorded vote. Knowing which committees function as graveyards — and which chairs control them — tells you more about a bill's real chances than any floor speech. You can trace any bill's committee path yourself in the bills database: open a bill to see every stop it made and where it stalled.

Figures are cumulative for 1996–2026, drawn from the Impact Lab's bills database (source: nmlegis.gov). Committee names and jurisdictions have changed over the period; counts reflect the committee codes as recorded. See our methodology.

— Amy Traylor, Editor & Publisher, New Mexico Impact Lab
